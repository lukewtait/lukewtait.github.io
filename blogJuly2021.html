General update Jan-July 2021

Wow, it's hard to believe it's been six months since my last blog post! It's been a busy few months, so today's post is just going to be a quick news blast on some have highlights from the past 6 months.

Moving to Birmingham University

The biggest news is my move from Cardiff University to the University of Birmingham. In February, I joined the Centre for Systems Modelling and Quantitative Biomedicine (SMQB) lead by Prof John Terry as a Research Fellow. In this role, I am working with the award-winning medical start-up Neuronostics to develop methods for aiding clinicians to diagnose epilepsy from cheap and non-invasive brain scans. This is a really exciting opportunity, and it's great to be back in the office and seeing people in person again!

Two new publications

I have had two new publications in the past few months. The first was entitled "A systematic evaluation of source reconstruction of resting MEG of the human brain with a new high-resolution atlas: Performance, precision, and parcellation" and was published in Human Brain Mapping. In this study, we evaluated different methods of 'source reconstruction', which means estimating what the brain activity looked like during a brain scan such as magneto- or electro- encephalography (M/EEG).

Source reconstruction is the estimation of the brain activity that generates the magnetic/electric fields recorded by M/EEG.

The second publication is still in press, but is entitled "A large-scale brain network mechanism for seizure propensity in Alzheimer's disease" and was accepted in PLoS Computational Biology. In this study, we used a mathematical model of the brain to try and understand better why people with Alzheimer's disease are more likely to develop seizures. I discussed this paper in more detail in my last blog post.

Methodology used to build a brain model and test why people with Alzheimer's are more likely to develop seizures.

Preprint(s) and toolbox: M/EEG Cortical Microstates

In addition to two papers, we have released two new pre-prints and a MATLAB toolbox based around brain microstates. To learn more about brain microstates, see my Research page. Traditionally, brain microstates can only be recorded using a low-resolution imaging method (called EEG) on the surface of the scalp. We have developed a method to use a higher-resolution method near the scalp (called MEG), or also to obtain microstates on the brain surface (using source reconstruction). This gives us more detailed insight into which parts of the brain are important for the different brain states.

Classic microstates vs our method. (Left) The 4 classic EEG microstates. (Right) 10 brain microstates found using our method. Our method allows us to see which parts of the brain are associated with each state.

In our first preprint, entitled "MEG cortical microstates: spatiotemporal characteristics, dynamic functional connectivity and stimulus-evoked responses", we describe the method and some results in real brain data.

In our second preprint, entitled "+microstate: A MATLAB toolbox for brain microstate analysis in sensor and cortical EEG/MEG", we describe a set of codes (i.e. a "toolbox") which are freely available for anyone to use in order to use our methods. The preprint and toolbox come with tutorials and descriptions of how the codes work.
