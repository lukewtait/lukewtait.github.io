Imaging the brain

What is neuroimaging?

"Neuroimaging" refers to generating a picture of the brain. Structural neuroimaging involves generating a picture of the structure of the brain. Conversely functional neuroimaging images the activity of the brain over time. My research focuses primarily on functional neuroimaging, in particular magneto- and electro-encephalography (MEG/EEG respectively). When a mass of neurons within a region of the brain synchronously become active, they create electric and magentic fields around the head. MEG/EEG place sensors outside the head (MEG) or on the scalp (EEG) and record these magnetic/electric fields.

Structural and functional neuroimaging. In black and white is an MRI scan showing the structure of the brain (structural imaging), whilst overlaid (yellow/red pixels) shows which parts of the brain are active at a single time point (functional imaging).

Why is functional neuroimaging important?

Functional neuroimaging is crucial to understand the brain activity underpinning healthy cognition and neurological disorders. For example, questions that can be answered with functional neuroimaging include "which areas of the brain become active during a certain task?", or "how do different areas of the brain work together?", and how is this activity dysfunctional in disease? Functional neuroimaging is also useful as a diagnostic tool in the clinic. For example, at present EEG is used to diagnose epilepsy. I am interested in adapting EEG to aid with diagnosing Alzheimer's disease at stages before a diagnosis can be made based on cognitive symptoms.

What is "source reconstruction"?

In the figure above, the functional data was recorded from EEG. But if EEG is recorded using sensors placed on the scalp, then why is the activity not shown on the surface of the head? The answer is because source reconstruction was applied to the EEG. Source reconstruction is the process of estimating where in the brain the M/EEG activity originated from.

Source reconstruction is the estimation of the brain activity that generates the magnetic/electric fields recorded by M/EEG.

Unfortunately, there is no unique solution to source reconstruction, because there are many many more neurons in the brain (86 billion) than sensors in MEG (typically 200-300) or EEG (typically 10-300). This is known as an underdetermined problem, and is like asking "If A+B=10, what are A and B?". This question, like the source reconstruction problem, has an infinite number of solutions. I am interested in how we can make a 'best estimate' of the source activity.

Where are meaningful "regions of the brain" to study?: An "atlas" of the brain

Once we have imaged the brain, it is useful to define regions of the brain to study. To get the most from our data, these regions should be defined meaningfully and not just chosen at random. This is often done using a map of brain regions, called an atlas. Probably the most detailed atlas available to date was developed by the Human Connectome Project by looking at brain structure, function and activation during a range of tasks, and connectivity. We have developed an atlas designed for use with MEG based on the Human Connectome Project's atlas. This atlas is shown below on a smoothed brain surface.

Imaging at smaller scales

The focus of this page has been on MEG and EEG, which are methods of imaging the whole brain, and are the primary focus of my recent research. However, there are many other methods for functional imaging of the brain, at a range of spatial scales. I have experience working with a range of other methods, primarily in rodent models of Alzheimer's disease. During my PhD, I spent six months working in a patch clamp lab, recording the activity of single neurons. I additionally have interest in modalities such as invasive local field potential recordings, which are similar to EEG but are recorded using electrodes placed within the brain tissue, and two-photon calcium imaging, which records the activity in a group of neurons by imaging the calcium dynamics of each neuron in the group. By combining neuroimaging at multiple scales, a more detailed description of the brain can be obtained.
