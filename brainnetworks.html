Brain networks

What is a network?

The mathematical study of networks is called graph theory. A network (or graph) consists of a set of objects (nodes), which are connected by edges. In graph theory, it doesn't matter what the nodes are - they can be Facebook profiles in an online social network, animals in a food web, or (masses of) neurons in a brain - what is important is how nodes are connected, and (in the case of a weighted network) strongly connected they are.

What is a network? On the left, we show a generic network structure. This network consists of five nodes (black circles), and lines represent edges between the nodes. On the right, we show an example network (the New York subway). Here, stations (white circles) are nodes, and the train lines running between stations are the edges. What are brain networks?

The human brain consists of 86 billion neurons, which communicate through electrical activity. These electrical signals are passed between neurons via structures called synapses. Therefore we can consider the set of neurons as nodes, and synapses as edges. This is an example of a brain network. Such a huge network is often intractable to study mathematically, however, so usually we construct networks on different spatial scales depending on what scale we wish to study. See the figure below for examples.

Brain networks at multiple spatial scales. (A) At the microscale, neurons are wired up through synaptic connections. Groups of neurons with similar properties (represented by colours in the figure), are organized in layers. (B) At the meso-scale, we can consider populations of neurons with similar properties as a neural mass, and the connectivity between neural masses has weights dependent on the number and strength of connections between neurons within the population. (C) At the whole brain macro-scale, we can consider different regions of the brain as a neural mass. The macro-scale network is the density of connections between regions of the brain. Figure and caption modified from Tait et al (2019) Doctoral thesis.

What are functional brain networks?

Brain networks don't just have to be defined byphysical connections. Functional brain networks use statistical measures of synchrony to generate a network. Consider two neurons - if they fire signals at the same time, we say there is functional connectivity between these neurons. By recording the activity of a group of neurons, and calculating a statistical measure of similarity between their activity, we can construct a functional brain network (see figure below). Functional networks can be calculated on all the same spatial scales as the structural networks, shown in the figure above.

Functional connectivity between neurons. This figure shows the novel method used in chapter 2 of Tait (2019) Doctoral thesis to calculate synchrony between time courses of neuronal activity. In A, activity is recorded from a set of neurons using two-photon calcium imaging. Red stars show times at which the neurons become active. B-D show a method of cleaning the data get a more accurate estimation of synchrony (when one cell fires, does the other?). E-F show networks. Each column/row is a node of the network (a neuron), whilst each coloured block is the edge between a pair of neurons.

Why study brain networks?

Brain networks are crucial for cognition. The classical view of the brain is that different areas are responsible for different cognitive processes - for example, there is a part of the brain responsible for vision (the visual cortex) and a part responsible for movement (the motor cortex). But now consider walking down the street. Some communication is required between vision and movement so that we don't walk into a lampost. For higher cognitive processes, integration between the brain's processing systems is crucial.

Using network science to study the brain can help us understand why and how it is connected, and how cognitive impairment may arise when a breakdown of synapses occurs in the brain. An example from my research is shown below with respect to Alzheimer's disease.

Network science can help explain cognitive impairment in Alzheimer's disease. In Tait et al. (2019), we studied the network substrates of cognitive impairment in Alzheimer's disease. We calculated functional networks, and used network science to study the networks. We found the temporal lobes, which are a language centre of the brain, became disconnected in people with Alzheimer's disease. In the people with Alzheimer's disease, the degree of disconnection directly correlated with reduced cognitive test scores (using the mini-mental state exam, MMSE), and particularly the language score. Hence, network science applied to brain networks has shown how disruption to the language network is related to cognitive impairment in Alzheimer's disease.
